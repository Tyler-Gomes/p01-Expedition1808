Expedition, 1808

Story

The expedition departed on May 14, 1808 and by late November your party has traversed hundreds of miles worth of river and plains. You have met and traded with many Native people, relations with the Natives thus far has been peaceful. As the Corps moves off of the plains you see mountains ahead. The mountains are already snowcapped preventing any passage until spring so Captain Lewis suggests the company set up camp for the winter. The majority of the men are assisting Lewis in building the winter fort; you are assigned a group of men to lead on a hunting party. Due to the amount of ammunition packed hunting for food daily is the norm. In preparation for the winter you are attempting to take as much game as possible. On your second day of hunting, your hunting party is shocked to find that one member of the crew is missing. You order the remaining men to spread out and begin searching. At that moment you hear a single shot, followed by a volley of gunfire fired in return. It becomes obvious that there is a large group of hostile Natives nearby; the men look to you for a command.

CHOICES

  • You decide to go after the Natives in hope of recovering your missing member. The amount of gunfire that has occurred no doubt alerted the back at the fort that there is trouble. You send a runner back to the fort to tell the rest of the Corp will organize into small militia to assist you in fighting the Natives. This battle will likely expend the additional ammunition and you will undoubtedly lose men but it will prevent turning the area into hostile woods too dangerous for hunting. Even though a few men will become casualties this will allow you to properly prepare for the winter.
  • You tell the men to split up and move silently back to the fort. You will tell Captain Lewis of the Native force and to prepare for a siege. By choosing not to engage the Indians you cede the woods to the Natives as Lewis will no longer allow hunting parties into the woods to avoid further loses. The food for the winter will have to be caught in the nearby river, however the catch is unlikely to be enough. By not fighting the Natives now you preserve hope for peaceful relations in the spring.
