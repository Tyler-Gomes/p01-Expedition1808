Expedition, 1808

You have been riding north for about a month without a trace of the western passage. One morning you notice that there is campfire smoke on the horizon. You and your men move cautiously towards it. Upon approach you are confronted by a Native lookout. You quickly realize that he is a member of the same Oar tribe that offered you assistance in the spring. The lookout brings you into camp and you begin asking about the western passage. The elders of the Oar tribe agree to lead you and your men to the western passage. After a short ride you come upon a river of epic scale. You estimate that it is two miles wide. The Natives assist you in constructing boats for the voyage home. As you depart eastward down the passage you think back to all the men that you have lost upon the way. After a few weeks of sailing the passage you begin seeing more and more recognizable terrain. Pretty soon you enter the Grand Lakes of the Northern Republics. You realize that you are home. As news spreads about the western passage thousands of American settlers scramble westward hoping to stake out a new life. The surviving men of the Frontier Corps and you are hailed as national heroes.
